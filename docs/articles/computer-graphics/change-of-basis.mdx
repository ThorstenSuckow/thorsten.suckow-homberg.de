## Standard Basis and Identity Matrix

Let $\vec{u}, \vec{v}, \vec{w}$ be orthonormal vectors of the standard basis in $\mathbb{R}^3$

$$
\begin{alignat*}{3}

\vec{u} &= \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\\
\vec{v} &= \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\\
\vec{w} &= \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\\

\end{alignat*}
$$

and $\vec{p}$ be an arbitrary vector $\begin{pmatrix} p_x \\ p_y \\ p_z\end{pmatrix}$.

We can write $\vec{p}$ as the matrix-vector product $\boldsymbol{A}p = p$, where $\boldsymbol{A}$ is a $3 \times 3$-matrix where the rows represent the vectors $\vec{u}, \vec{v}, \vec{w}$:

$$
\begin{alignat*}{3}

\begin{pmatrix}
 1 & 0 & 0 \\
 0 & 1 & 0 \\
 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} p_x \\ p_y \\ p_z\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \vec{u} \cdot \vec{p} \\ \vec{v} \cdot \vec{p} \\ \vec{w} \cdot \vec{p} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} p_x \\ p_y \\ p_z\end{pmatrix}
\end{alignat*}
$$

It is immediately clear that the resulting vector contains the components of $p$ parallel to the respective axes of the **coordinate-frame** represented by $\boldsymbol{A}$:

$$
(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{p}) \vec{u} = (\frac{\vec{u} \cdot \vec{p}}{|\vec{u}|^2}) \vec{u} =  (\frac{\vec{u} \cdot \vec{p}}{1}) \vec{u} = (p_x) \vec{u} = (p_x, 0, 0)^T
$$

Analogusly for $\vec{v}, \vec{w}$, we rewrite $\vec{p}$ as a linear combination of

$$
p = (\vec{u} \cdot \vec{p}) \vec{u} + (\vec{v} \cdot \vec{p}) \vec{v} + (\vec{w} \cdot \vec{p}) \vec{w} = \begin{pmatrix}p_x \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix}0 \\ p_y \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} +\begin{pmatrix}0 \\ 0 \\ p_z \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}p_x \\ p_y\\ p_z \end{pmatrix}
$$


This holds for any vector $p'$ of any subspace of $\mathbb{R}^3$ that undergoes a change of basis, which we will show in the following.

## Change of Basis with Orthonormal Axes

**Claim**:
Let $A$ be a $3 \times 3$ matrix with its rows representing vectors $\vec{u}, \vec{v}, \vec{w}$ such that

$$
\begin{alignat*}{3}
&|\vec{u}| = |\vec{v}| = |\vec{w}| = 1 \\
& \vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = 0 \\
& \vec{u} \cdot \vec{w} = 0 \\
& \vec{v} \cdot \vec{w} = 0
\end{alignat*}
$$

Hence, $\vec{u}, \vec{v}, \vec{w}$ is an **orthonormal** basis in $\mathbb{R}^3$

Then the matrix-vector product

$$
\boldsymbol{A}p = p'
$$

is a vector $$p'$$ where the components are the parallel-projections of the respective components onto the axes of the orthonormal basis.

## Proof by Scalar Products:

The multiplicatione
$$
\begin{alignat*}{3}

\begin{pmatrix}
 u_x & u_y & u_z \\
 v_x & v_y & v_z \\
 w_x & w_y & w_z \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} p_x \\ p_y \\ p_z\end{pmatrix} \end{alignat*}
$$

yields the vector

$$
p' = \begin{pmatrix}
 u_x p_x + u_y p_y + u_z p_z \\
 v_x p_x + v_y p_y + v_z p_z \\
 w_x p_x + w_y p_y + w_z p_z \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \vec{u} \cdot \vec{p} \\ \vec{v} \cdot \vec{p} \\ \vec{w} \cdot \vec{p}\end{pmatrix}
$$

Intuitively, the components in the resulting vector $p'$ show the _scalar amount_ the component are showing into the respective direction. To prove that the direction if the components is actually belonging to the directions defined by $\vec{u}, \vec{v}, \vec{w}$, we rewrite $p'$ as follows


$$
p' = (\vec{u} \cdot \vec{p})\vec{u} +  (\vec{v} \cdot \vec{p})\vec{v} + (\vec{w} \cdot \vec{p})\vec{w}
$$

The dot product of $p'$ and the respective vectors will leave the vector in the equation and remove those perpendicular to the vector. We show that the dot product of $p'$ and any vector of the orthogonal basis removes any component other than the component defined by the combination of $p$ and the respective vector:


$$
\begin{alignat*}{3}
p' \cdot \vec{u} &= (\vec{u} \cdot \vec{p})(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{u}) +  (\vec{v} \cdot \vec{p})(\vec{v}  \cdot \vec{u}) + (\vec{w} \cdot \vec{p})(\vec{w}  \cdot \vec{u})\\
&= (\vec{u} \cdot \vec{p})(1) +  (\vec{v} \cdot \vec{p})(0) + (\vec{w} \cdot \vec{p})(0) \\
& = \vec{u} \cdot \vec{p}
\end{alignat*}
$$
